### Lesson 1: The Marketplace Landscape

Welcome! Before you start building, you need to understand the landscape you're entering. The marketplace approach differs dramatically from a traditional single-platform strategy, and understanding why matters for every decision you'll make. Each marketplace has its own economics, rules, competition levels, and customer base. Selling on multiple platforms isn't just more work — it's a fundamentally different strategy.

#### SESSION 1 — UNDERSTAND THE MARKETPLACE ECOSYSTEM

When you list on a marketplace, you're renting shelf space in someone else's store. That store already has traffic, trust, and buying intent. Your job is to show up in the right marketplace for your product. Different marketplaces attract different customers, have different fee structures, and reward different kinds of sellers. Understanding the full landscape helps you make better decisions about where to invest your time and money — and more importantly, which platform is right to start on.

### Lesson 2: Platform Comparison Framework

This is your decision-making tool. Don't get yourself in a platform battle — use this comparison framework before committing to any marketplace. The goal is to find the right platform for your specific situation.

#### THE PLATFORM DECISION FRAMEWORK

Since every marketplace suits different sellers, use this framework to make an educated comparison and choose wisely. Think about these dimensions when comparing any two or three marketplaces.

#### Barrier to Entry

How hard is it to get started on this platform? Some platforms allow a seller account in minutes; others take weeks or months for approval.

#### Competition in Niche

How many sellers are competing in your niche? You can use free tools to assess the competitive landscape on any major platform.

#### Fee Structure

What does it actually cost to sell? Fee structures range from a few percent to 15%+. On some platforms there are also listing fees, monthly fees, and more.

#### Traffic Quality

What kind of buyer arrives on this platform? Some platforms have high purchase intent; others require you to bring your own traffic.

#### Scale & Speed

Once your product is listed, how fast can the platform grow a new seller? Platform customers trust listings and often buy from sellers they've never heard of.

## Module 2: Choosing Your Business Model

Learn the three core e-commerce business models, assess your available capital and time, then make a confident, informed business model decision.

### Lesson 1: Business Models Deep-Dive

Now that you've started a business with this course, it's time to make the most important decision you'll make as an e-commerce seller. Your business model determines your margins, your risk, your operational requirements, and your income ceiling. This is why we spend an entire lesson on it.

#### The Three Core Business Models

There are three main ways to sell products online (we'll ignore the dropshipping-only model since it's increasingly difficult in today's regulated marketplace environment). Each of these three models has a very different entry point, risk, and reward profile.

#### Retail Arbitrage — The Low-Cost Entry Point

With retail arbitrage, you source products from retail stores at a discount and resell them for a profit. You buy, for example, clearance items at 30% off retail and sell them at full retail price on Amazon.

#### Wholesale — The Knowledge Gateway

The value gateway. Wholesale has been approved very precisely by brand-based wholesale sellers. You purchase products from brands or distributors in bulk at wholesale pricing — usually 50%+ off retail — then resell on your chosen platform. There is no risk once you understand it, but getting started requires more capital, more research, and more preparation.

#### Private Label — The Brand Builder

Private labelling means creating your own branded products. You find a generic or unbranded product in a niche, source it (usually from overseas manufacturers), put your brand name on it, and sell it as your own. This is the highest-risk, highest-reward model.

#### Amazon FBA Bundling — The Hybrid

The best of all worlds, leveraged for short-term results. You can combine two or more existing products into a custom bundle, price the bundle above the individual products' sum, and sell the bundle on Amazon as your own unique listing. Very best for scaling quickly.

#### Dropship — The Growing Startup

With dropshipping, you don't hold any inventory. You list a product on an e-commerce platform, and when a customer purchases it, you order directly from a supplier who ships directly to them. You're essentially a

middleman with a margin.

### Lesson 2: Capital & Time Assessment

The most common mistake new sellers make is choosing a business model based on what sounds exciting rather than what actually fits their situation. This lesson fixes that. We're going to do an honest assessment of what you actually have available to work with.

#### Your Capital Reality

Capital refers to the money you have available to invest in your business. It's the single most important factor — more than your skills, your niche, or your motivation — because it determines what's physically possible for you.

#### Under $500

Retail arbitrage is your path. You can start sourcing immediately, build small, and scale with selling profits.

#### $500–$2,000

This range opens up affiliate marketing as a parallel income while you're also able to begin online arbitrage (sourcing products online using a phone or computer).

#### $2,000–$5,000

Wholesale becomes available at this stage. Your first wholesale account typically requires a minimum order amount. You can begin product label research and start placing your wholesale orders here.

#### $5,000–$15,000

All business models are now available. Most people find getting serious traction easier in this range. You can test private label properly, run a real wholesale strategy, or experiment with multiple models simultaneously.

#### $15,000+

Full acceleration is possible. You can now run large-scale product testing, carry more inventory, grow monthly compounding, and launch multiple products at once.

#### Your Time Reality

Time matters just as much as capital. The more time you have available, the faster you'll grow — but even sellers with limited time can build a substantial business with the right model.

Under 5 hours per week Retail arbitrage is the right choice. You source when you can, ship when you can, and list when you have time.

5–10 hours per week You can properly run retail arbitrage with some serious growth trajectory. You can manage online arbitrage by sourcing at home in the evenings, list products multiple times per week.

10–20 hours per week

Your platform choices are fully open. You can build systematically. Wholesale, private label, and bundling all become viable.

20–40 hours per week At this level, you're operating like a business. That means you can implement anything we cover in this course — and potentially hire your first virtual assistant within 60–90 days.

### Lesson 2: Competition Analysis & Validation

Now that you have your research tools from Lesson 1 — the next step is to understand how to use a real competitive analysis, build a picture of the competition on the market, and know how to identify the type of competition that allows you to make a sustained competitive advantage over the long run.

#### Read the Competitive Landscape

The best place to start your competitive analysis is 5 star review mapping. You pull out all of the competitor's 5 star reviews to find what they're doing right, then look for patterns in 1 star reviews to identify common customer complaints. You'll always find some simple validated truth: Sellers with Amazon's 10,000 reviews have taken several years and thousands of orders to get there. If their data started at 1,750 reviews per year, you know they've been on the platform for roughly 6 years. If they started much earlier, then you know your product is established enough to have demand.

#### The Validation Framework

The methods you learned from Lesson 1 should build you a 5-round answer from a 3-round structured. Take data from at least 3–5 different competitors before making a final decision. Then look at: your margin assumptions, your competition analysis, and whether the data supports a reasonable entry point. If there is more than 65% of competitors below 25% market share, then the market is fragmented and the market is open for competition.

#### Extreme Validation Pattern

Selling by new product validation. Then the market has analytics data sorted by date. Only use the raw data you collect, it is a dataset but not yet complete and full source data.

### Lesson 2: Negotiation & Supplier Terms

The fundamental secret to this lesson — and a fact most e-commerce training glosses over — is that all business decisions are supplier relationships. This lesson covers the art and science of supplier negotiation — how to ask for the right terms, and what kinds of terms to focus on.

#### MODULE: NEGOTIATE LIKE AN EXPERIENCED BUYER

Every order you place is a negotiation. That's true, if you place one order or a thousand. Every supplier relationship is a term-based negotiation and it's always worth being a professional negotiator in all of your business.

#### FIVE TERMS THAT MATTER MOST:

#### 1. Unit Price

The most obvious term. You want the best unit price per item that you can reasonably negotiate. This supplier agreement is the baseline for your entire margin calculation, so master it first.

#### 2. Minimum Order Quantity (MOQ)

Suppliers set a MOQ — often 100–500+ units for wholesale. Negotiate this lower when starting out, especially if you're testing a new product. Many suppliers will agree to trial orders.

#### 3. Payment Terms

Clarify up front: full payment in advance (risky), 30% deposit and 70% on delivery (standard), or net payment terms (advanced). More favourable terms free up your cash flow between orders.

#### 4. Shipping & Lead Time

Clarify the shipping window and carrier options. From the lead time, you'll be able to manage your inventory running below the stock level threshold so that you'll never run out of stock.

#### 5. Open to Exclusivity — Will they?

If the supplier will grant you exclusivity, that's a deal. If the supplier is trying to protect their own wholesale listing, exclusivity is the most important deal you have.

### Lesson 1: Listing Anatomy

Time to build. You've set up your store, and now the key tool in this business — your product listing — needs to be constructed properly. This lesson shows you the anatomy of a high-converting listing and the key components that directly drive sales.

#### MODULE 6: CREATE A LISTING THAT CONVERTS

A listing has five core components, each of which has a direct effect on how buyers make purchase decisions in the marketplace:

#### 1. TITLE

Your first and most important selling tool. On every marketplace, it is the primary visibility factor. Titles should be keyword-rich, clear, specific, and 100–200 characters for Amazon; shorter for Etsy or eBay.

#### 2. IMAGES

You get 6–9 image spots. Use them all. Always start with a clean product photo on a white background. Then add a lifestyle image. Next add a feature highlight image. Then size/dimensions. Then comparison. Max quality images are essential.

#### 3. BULLETS / FEATURES

Customers don't read listings — they scan. Use 3–5 bullet points that highlight the key features and address the top objections that customers are likely to have. Start with a bold, capitalised header followed by the benefit.

#### 4. DESCRIPTION

The description goes deeper. This is where you explain what your product is, what it does, how it works, and why it's better. The description section can include key comparisons and benefit statements.

#### 5. BACKEND KEYWORDS (Amazon)

Backend keywords are not visible to customers but are indexed by the Amazon search algorithm. You can list additional keywords here that you didn't fit in your title or bullets.

## Module 7: Fulfillment & Operations

### Lesson 1: Fulfillment Models Explained

Now that you have a listing live, you need to decide how your product will actually get to your customer. The question is: who handles storage, packing, and shipping? This module covers the fulfillment models available to you and helps you choose the right one.

#### MODULE: BUILD YOUR FULFILLMENT SYSTEM

There are four different fulfillment methods, each with different cost, speed, and complexity:

#### 1. IN-STOCK INVENTORY (FBA)

You keep inventory yourself. You buy the physical product at first order — you decide when to buy it. You also use it for PRIME listings. Then, Full Fulfilment takes over. Standard FBA creates a listing that ships within 48 hours and is eligible for PRIME.

#### 2. FBA (Fulfilled by Amazon)

You send inventory to Amazon's warehouse. You keep inventory in the Amazon fulfilment channel and sell on your Amazon listing as well as via the full Amazon standard setup. Amazon handles all of the fulfilment tasks from the order point onward, including the Prime badge.

#### 3. FBM (Fulfilled by Merchant)

You ship products yourself from your own location to the customer. You can then track inventory within a multi-platform category; then manage and ship orders from your own location; store items in your home — but it's the most flexible.

#### 4. DROPSHIP/3PL (3rd Party)

You outsource to Suppliers; Replicators; Fulfillment centres; or Platform specific stores. They charge you more in most cases to do it, but you don't hold inventory. Usually works with multi-fulfillment channel solutions like ShipBob, Deliverr.

#### 5. PRINT-ON-DEMAND (JUST)

Custom print makes it where only one product is completed when a customer orders it — no inventory is ever stored anywhere. You don't even need to handle storage if you don't want to. Good for specialty items like custom designs, but comes with higher per-unit cost.

### Lesson 1: Performance Analytics & KPIs

You've got data. Every sale is a data point. Every click is a data point. The sellers who win long term are those who understand their numbers and then make data-driven decisions to improve them. This module starts with the essential KPIs every seller should track consistently.

#### MODULE 8: KNOW YOUR NUMBERS

The Performance metrics sellers who grow profitably are the ones who group their metrics into your simple dashboard. Once you know these numbers, your sellers can look at their complete dashboard and make decisions faster — the core KPIs you should track.

#### KEY METRICS TO TRACK DAILY

#### 1. REVENUE

Revenue means setting all sales listing records from your account; calculate Total Net Revenue: Platform fees subtracted, your Target KPIs = revenue / month.

#### 2. MARGINS (PROFITABILITY)

Profitability means setting a margin per product; calculate the profit margin of each product/category. Track with the formula: Revenue / 100 (Profit Margin). Platform fees: Shipping fees + Tax/Duty = 11 TP3.

#### 3. ADVERTISING COST OF SALES (ACOS)

Advertising costs means tracking your advertising costs on all platform selling: calculate (ad revenue / total ad cost × 100 = ACOS). If advertising budgets are 2% of all sales, that's $20 per year ad spend.

#### 4. INVENTORY TURN-OVER

Inventory turnovers means tracking your returns by monitoring your items and turns: calculate your returns: 30-day turns, full turns to find turnover data effectively.

#### 5. RETURNS RATE

Returns data means finding your product returns. Review: A rating above a certain level is a warning or an issue that matters. Rating: a high return rate may signal a product or listing issue.

#### 6. CUSTOMER FEEDBACK SCORE

Your rating across platforms: Rating of 5 stars always signals a reliable, trustworthy seller. Building a consistent 5-star score is particularly profitable.

### Lesson 2: Inventory & Pricing Across Platforms

The single number-one challenge of multi-marketplace selling is inventory. You'll soon find out that you've sold products on more than one platform. Constantly, this becomes an issue because you've sold products on one platform and you've run out of stock on another. This lesson covers the three strategies for managing inventory and pricing across platforms.

#### MODULE 9B: SYNC YOUR INVENTORY

#### THE OVERSELL PROBLEM

The oversell of multi-marketplace is the number one reason why sellers lose their best-performing products. Someone has a lot of multi-platform inventory at one time — really especially when you are on FBA status — the stock of one platform overrides the stock on all. This means you have more than one live listing when you run out of stock — just the reverse.

#### STRATEGY 1 — JUST STAY AHEAD (Manual)

A basic level inventory app specifically ensures a basic daily sales check. You have a spreadsheet and update it daily. Pro: Ultra-simple, free to maintain. Con: Manually tracking. Risk of multiple-listing errors.

#### STRATEGY 2 — AVAILABLE APP SYNC (Intermediate)

At the intermediate level, you connect your multi-marketplace platforms to an inventory management platform like ListingMirror, Linnworks, or ChannelAdvisor. These platforms use actual syncing real-time between the platforms. Pro: Automated sync. Con: Monthly cost approximately $50–100 per month. Requires careful setup.

#### STRATEGY 3 — AUTOMATED SYNC (Advanced)

Use true multi-channel inventory software like Listing Juice, Shipo, or Linnworks, ChannelAdvisor to the listing platforms and keep the fulfillment data accurate to track orders, sales, and inventory on one platform above all others. Pro: Hands off, multi-platforms. Con: Monthly $100-300+.
